Key findings

  • After removing inflation, the market's price has grown about 2.5% a year. Most of the apparent growth comes from inflation and reinvested dividends (neither in this price series).
  • Valuation has tracked the next decade's returns: a higher CAPE has generally been followed by lower 10-year real returns (R² ≈ 0.11). The signal is real but moderate — the headline p ≈ 1e-40 is inflated by overlapping 10-year windows; with Newey-West errors it is ≈0.003 and on non-overlapping decades (~13 in 150 years) it is not significant. The latest valid CAPE (≈2023, as the series lags) is near the most expensive ~5% of readings since 1881.
  • Large losses recur throughout the record. The worst real price drawdown reached −80.6%, and the VIX spikes during every crisis.
  • Gold's average correlation with stocks is close to zero, so it has historically helped cushion the deepest drawdowns — though a low average is no guarantee in any single crash.
Recommendation: For a long-term investor, the data leans toward setting return expectations around below-average real returns given today's high valuation (a moderate signal, not a guarantee), diversifying with low-correlation assets, and automating contributions and rebalancing.
